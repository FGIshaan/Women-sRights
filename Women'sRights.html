Equal Pay & Women's Rights

Many women can be seen at an outdoor event in this image supporting each, demanding for equal rights in the workplace and demanding for the same number of wages for the same amount of work. The Equal Pay Case of 1969 tried to get rid of any discriminatory laws against women in the workplace. These laws made sure that women can find and maintain employment. New laws were enacted to ensure that men and women are treated and compensated equally.

Two women can be seen in this image who are holding signs/posters trying to raise awareness about the pay gap between men and women for doing the same job in the workplace. Doing this ensures that men and women are both treated with respect and have a value in the workplace. The movement began because women were paid less than men for doing the same work and were not treated equally. The main reason for this was that women were getting paid less for doing the same task because of their gender. The main motive for this movement was to ensure that women get the same pay as men for doing the same job as them in the workplace. Protestors like these were important for the equal pay movement because they not just raised awareness about the topic, but they also encouraged other women who were not a part of the movement to join them. The unjust treatment of people based on their gender in the workplace was attempted to put an end to by the 1984 Sex Discrimination Act.

Important events in this image includes the 1969 Equal Pay Case, which tried to remove any discriminatory laws equal pay; the 1984 Sex Discrimination Act, which attempted to get rid of gender discrimination; and the 1986 Affirmative Action legislation, which promoted equal opportunity. These new legislations have had a significant impact on how women are treated at work. It ensured that women were treated and compensated equally to men.
